Ruminations and remnants / how to love yourself

Last week I talked about why you should love yourself and today I will share some thoughts and tips on practicing self-compassion based on my personal experiences. I'm no expert in self-love; in fact, I'm quite far from it. But because it was such a challenge, maybe what worked for me may also work for you. So, here's how to love yourself when society and family may have conditioned you otherwise.

Mere & Mirror Exposure Effect

Use a mirror and the mere exposure effect to learn to love yourself. Mere exposure effect refers to the phenomenon for the increased liking of a stimulus following repeated exposure to that stimulus (a.k.a. the familiarity principle)1. Since you primarily perceive the world inside-looking-out and rarely perceive yourself (unless you're Narcissus I guess), the advice you may have heard [targeting women] to look in the mirror and say one thing you love about yourself everyday is actually founded in science. It might feel weird at first, but like with all new skills, it will feel less shamefully awkward and more self-affirmative over time. In the wise words of Sheryl Lee Ralph:

"People don't have to like you, people don't have to love you, they don't even have to respect you. But when you look in the mirror, you better love what you see."2

Know thyself to love thyself

If you don't really know something or someone and don't fully perceive it in its truest form, do you technically actually love it? You may love a version of it based on your skewed perceptions or projections, but you wouldn't really be loving the subject in its genuine form. Scholars argue that this is the case for the self as well, as self-love requires that you know and understand yourself better3. We can only love ourselves if we know and accept ourselves as we are. And, to know yourself, it all starts with—unsurprisingly—mindfulness4, 5, 6.

Show up for yourself

"You are your own worst critic" is a common phrase that holds true for many. For people with self-critical personalities, hearing generic insubstantial advice telling us we need to be kinder to ourselves because it's good for you7 or because you're special or unique are not sufficient motivators. These advice can sound self-delusional and dishonest. Instead, what feels more honest might be to confront your recurring thoughts/behaviors that trigger your vicious cycles of self-loathing. Only then can you identify the core values or expectations that you're failing meet, which then instigate these toxic thought spirals. Once you've identified these values or expectations, you can then devise a plan to show up for yourself. Then, by consistently showing up for yourself, you will internalize that you can rely on yourself, and inevitably, you will start to love yourself. And when you fail—which, being human, you inevitably will—every time you get back up and try again, you will learn how resilient you are, which is hard not to love.

"I know who I am. I love who I am. I like what I do and I like how I do it. And I like my mistakes and I like the way I learn and I like the pace with which I learn my mistakes. I don't want to be anybody else but me. And by knowing this, I want to continue figure out who the fuck I am." 8

Parent yourself

Most of us (~62% of American adults), have had adverse childhood experiences9. Humans are imperfectno citation needed; just look around, so it goes without saying that parents will be imperfect. As such, modeling the Internal Family Systems (IFS) psychotherapy appraoch8 in order to parent ourselves. Peggy O'Mara said, "the way we talk to our children becomes their inner voice", which certainly was the case for me. By identifying these internalized criticisms from our parents when engaging in our internal dialogues, we can learn to replace them with nurturing self-talk that is consciously and carefully constructed based on our core values10

Confront the odds of your very existence

Dr. Ali Binazir calculated the odds of any one individual existing11 and the result was 1 in 102,685,000, "that's basically zero". He compared it to the odds of two million people rolling a trillion-sided die and all landing on the same number. He considered:

  • The odds of your parents meeting (~1 in 20,000)
  • The odds of them having children (~1 in 2,000)
  • The odds of the exact sperm and egg meeting (~1 in 400 quadrillion)
  • The odds of all your ancestors reproducing successfully for billions of years

If we also include the odds of Earth becoming a habitable planet with conditions suitable for complex life, it diminishes further. Some estimates based on Ward and Brownlee's model suggest the odds of an Earth-like planet emerging in the Milky Way are as low as 1 in 109 to 1 in 1012(meaning at most a handful of such planets in our galaxy)13 considering such a planet requires:
